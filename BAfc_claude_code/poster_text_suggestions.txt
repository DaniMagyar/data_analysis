SFN 2025 - Daniel Magyar et al.

TITLE: Functional diversity and sensory integration in the lateral and basal
amygdala nuclei during stimulus processing

================================================================================
INTRODUCTION (based on intro_sofar.docx and poster content)
================================================================================

The lateral amygdala (LA) is traditionally viewed as the primary site where
conditioned (CS) and unconditioned (US) stimuli converge during fear learning.
Thalamic nuclei (MGm/PIL) drive rapid LA responses (<20 ms latency) to both
auditory CS and footshock US, with ~10-30% of LA neurons forming the memory
engram through synaptic potentiation.

However, the conventional model assumes LA instructs downstream basal (BA) and
central (CeA) amygdala to generate defensive behaviors. Recent anatomical evidence
challenges this, suggesting LA may primarily target the amygdalo-striatal transition
area (AStria)—a region sharing thalamic and cortical inputs with LA but whose
role in fear processing remains unknown.

Using high-density silicon probes in awake mice, we recorded from LA, AStria, BA,
and CeA during fear conditioning. We characterized response diversity, temporal
dynamics of CS and US encoding, and tested how parvalbumin interneurons (PV INs)
gate information flow through optogenetic manipulation. Our findings reveal
unexpected functional organization that challenges the sequential processing model.

================================================================================
CONCLUSIONS (synthesizing poster results and intro_sofar.docx)
================================================================================

• LA and AStria form a functional unit: Both regions exhibit remarkably similar
  CS and US response profiles, proportions of selective neurons, and temporal
  dynamics—challenging the traditional LA→BA→CeA sequential model.

• Thalamic drive mediates rapid sensory responses: Monosynaptic inputs from MGm/PIL
  produce short-latency (<25 ms) responses in both LA and AStria, with similar
  proportions of CS-selective, US-selective, and multisensory neurons.

• BA shows functional specialization: Unlike LA/AStria, BA exhibits minimal CS
  responsiveness and predominant US selectivity, suggesting distinct roles in
  processing aversive unconditioned stimuli rather than predictive cues.

• PV interneurons powerfully gate sensory processing: Optogenetic silencing of
  PV INs enhances monosynaptic excitatory responses in both LA and AStria,
  revealing feedforward inhibitory control of thalamic information flow.

• CS-US integration occurs at the single-neuron level: Convergent inputs onto
  multisensory neurons provide a cellular substrate for associative learning
  across the extended amygdala network.

These results establish that AStria operates in parallel with LA during initial
fear learning, rather than receiving processed information from BA or CeA. This
parallel LA-AStria pathway may enable robust threat detection, while BA specialization
suggests a complementary role in processing unconditioned aversive stimuli. Our
findings necessitate re-evaluating information flow in the amygdala fear circuit.
